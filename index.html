Preliminary Exploration

A big focus of the group I'm in here at the media lab, Social Machines, is analyzing political discourse on social media. In a study done on Twitter by the non-profit associated with our group, Cortico, it was found that the Guardian leans slightly left. This made me think that the positive and negative phrasing surrounding each individual may differ a bit. I decided to first look at how much attention Hillary Clinton, Bernie Sanders, and Donald Trump each receieved between 1999 and 2017.

For reference, the data were scraped on 10/25/2017.

Good vs. Bad

The preliminary exploration was as expected: a spike in chatter for Clinton in 2008, and then a dramatic increase in talk about all three candidates between 2014-2016 as they announced intents of presidential campaigns. Since Trump, Clinton, and Sanders all are talked about by the Guardian to varying degrees between 1999 and 2017, I thought it'd be interesting to link back into the original idea that the site has a slight left lean and check the co-occurences of each candidate's name with the words 'good' and 'bad.' As we can see, the proportions for 'good' and 'bad' for each is approximately the same.

Diving Deeper

Of course, 'good' and 'bad' are not the only adjectives used to describe political figures. To get a fuller picture, I went into Google's word2vec word embeddings, which computes how similar various words are to one another across a corpus of three billion words, and pulled the most similar words to 'good' and 'bad', on the spectrum of 'good' being the exact antonym of 'bad.' For synonyms of good: I got: 'terrific', 'fantastic', 'tremendous', 'wonderful', 'excellent', 'phenomenal', 'incredible', 'awesome', 'amazing', and 'super'. For 'bad', the similar words were: 'terrible', 'horrible', 'horrendous', 'dreadful', 'horrific', 'awful', 'atrocious', 'horrifying', 'appalling', and 'disastrous'. They are sorted from bottom to top by increasing size of the total series.
